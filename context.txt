  SECTION 1: PROJECT VISION
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

ONE LINE SUMMARY:
  Train a drone navigation policy whose neural architecture is constrained
  by the actual wiring diagram of a fruit fly's visual system — then test
  whether 50 million years of biological evolution produced a better 
  inductive bias than random sparsity for robot navigation.

THE CORE IDEA:
  Standard deep RL for drones uses dense neural networks with millions of
  parameters. This project asks: what if instead of a dense black-box, we
  use a sparse network whose connectivity pattern is copied directly from
  the Drosophila melanogaster (fruit fly) connectome?

  A fruit fly:
    - Navigates complex 3D environments
    - Detects and avoids obstacles at high speed
    - Uses ~100,000 neurons total
    - Runs on 10 microwatts of power
    - Has a FULLY MAPPED brain (FlyWire, Princeton 2023)

  Our hypothesis: the fly's visual navigation circuit, shaped by 50 million
  years of evolution, encodes structural inductive biases that help a sparse
  policy network learn drone navigation faster and generalize better than
  a randomly sparse or dense network.

THE RESEARCH CONTRIBUTION:
  1. First application of real connectome topology as a policy network mask
     in deep reinforcement learning for drone navigation
  2. Systematic comparison: dense vs random sparse vs connectome-sparse
  3. Demonstration of sim-to-real viability of sparse connectome policies
  4. Energy efficiency analysis: sparse connectome vs standard approaches

ULTIMATE GOAL:
  A complete software pipeline from:
    Biologically-constrained RL training
             ↓
    Sim-to-real transfer
             ↓
    Physical drone deployment
  
  Hardware target: Custom PX4 quadrotor or DJI Tello (TBD based on resources)

  PHASE 4A — Pseudo-3D First-Person Renderer
  File: render_3d.py
  Hardware: M1 MacBook Air
  Estimated Time: 1 session
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  GOAL:
    Replace the 2D top-down view with a first-person perspective projection
    that shows the drone flying through a 3D-looking tunnel.

  TECHNIQUE:
    Perspective projection (no OpenGL, pure Pygame math):
    - Tunnel is drawn as a series of trapezoid "rings" shrinking toward
      a central vanishing point
    - Each ring = one time step ahead of the drone
    - Rings closer to drone = larger, rings further = smaller
    - Tunnel curves are encoded as horizontal offset of ring centers
    - Result looks like a retro racing game / sci-fi corridor

  VISUAL ELEMENTS:
    - First-person tunnel with vanishing point
    - Depth-faded wall colours (bright near, dark far)
    - Drone crosshair/reticle at screen center
    - Sensor rays projected into 3D perspective
    - Same HUD: sensor bars, connectome panel, episode stats
    - Action indicator (Left/Straight/Right with colour)

  SUCCESS CRITERIA:
    - Tunnel visibly curves left and right
    - Sensor rays show correctly in perspective
    - 30 FPS maintained on M1

  PHASE 5 — 3D Custom Gymnasium Environment
  File: env_3d.py
  Hardware: M1 MacBook Air
  Estimated Time: 2 sessions
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  GOAL:
    Replace the 1D lateral movement model with a proper 3D physics
    environment where the drone has full 6-DOF movement.

  STATE SPACE (observation):
    [
      # Depth sensors (12 rays in sphere pattern)
      d0, d1, d2, d3, d4, d5, d6, d7, d8, d9, d10, d11,  # 12 values
      # IMU
      vel_x, vel_y, vel_z,    # linear velocity   (3 values)

      # Relative position
      dist_to_center,          # distance from tunnel center (1 value)
      heading_error,           # angle offset from tunnel direction (1 value)
    ]
    Total observation: 20 values

  ACTION SPACE (continuous):
    Box(low=-1, high=1, shape=(4,))
    [roll_cmd, pitch_cmd, yaw_cmd, thrust_cmd]

  PHYSICS MODEL:
    - Simplified quadrotor dynamics (not full Navier-Stokes)
    - Drag, gravity, motor response time
    - dt = 0.05s (20Hz control loop)
    - Good enough for algorithm development

  TUNNEL GEOMETRY:
    - 3D cylindrical tunnel
    - Curves in X and Y (left/right AND up/down)
    - Variable radius (narrows in hard sections)
    - Procedurally generated per episode

  REWARD FUNCTION:
    +1.0   per step survived
    +0.5   bonus for staying near tunnel center
    -0.1   per step * distance from center (encourages centering)
    -100   crash into wall
    +50    reach end of tunnel (if applicable)

  DOMAIN RANDOMIZATION (built in from day 1):
    - Tunnel radius: uniform(30, 80) units
    - Curve intensity: uniform(0.1, 0.8)
    - Sensor noise: gaussian(0, sigma) where sigma ~ uniform(0, 0.05)
    - Physics drag: uniform(0.8, 1.2) × nominal
    - Initial position jitter: uniform(-5, 5) in all axes

  SUCCESS CRITERIA:
    - Random agent survives > 10 steps
    - Observation space correctly shaped
    - Domain randomization produces varied episodes

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  PHASE 6 — PPO Training Loop
  File: train_ppo.py
  Hardware: M1 MacBook Air (short runs) + Modal.com (long runs)
  Estimated Time: 2 sessions
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  GOAL:
    Replace REINFORCE with Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO).
    PPO is the industry standard for drone RL and handles:
      - Continuous action spaces
      - Noisy reward signals
      - High-variance 3D environments

  WHY PPO OVER REINFORCE:
    REINFORCE:
      - High variance gradients
      - Needs full episode before update
      - Unstable with continuous actions
      - Bad with sparse rewards

    PPO:
      - Low variance (clip ratio prevents large updates)
      - Updates every N steps (not full episode)
      - Stable with continuous actions
      - Industry standard (used by OpenAI, DeepMind)

  ALGORITHM:
    1. Collect T timesteps of experience using current policy
    2. Compute GAE advantages: A_t = sum(gamma^k * delta_{t+k})
    3. For K epochs, sample mini-batches and compute:
       a. Policy loss:  L_clip = min(r_t * A_t, clip(r_t, 1-ε, 1+ε) * A_t)
       b. Value loss:   L_vf = (V(s_t) - G_t)^2
       c. Entropy loss: L_ent = -entropy(π)  [encourages exploration]
       d. Total loss:   L = -L_clip + c1*L_vf - c2*L_ent
    4. Backprop through sparse connectome (dead synapses stay dead)
    5. Clip gradients, Adam step

  HYPERPARAMETERS (starting point):
    lr          = 3e-4
    gamma       = 0.99
    gae_lambda  = 0.95
    clip_eps    = 0.2
    value_coef  = 0.5
    entropy_coef= 0.01
    n_steps     = 2048    (steps per update)
    batch_size  = 64
    n_epochs    = 10      (epochs per update)
    max_grad_norm = 0.5

  SUCCESS CRITERIA:
    - Training stable (no exploding gradients)
    - Average reward consistently increasing over 100k steps
    - Drone survives > 500 steps in 3D tunnel

  PHASE 7 — Scaled Sparse Brain for 3D
  File: model_v2.py
  Hardware: M1 MacBook Air
  Estimated Time: 1 session
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  GOAL:
    Upgrade FlyPolicyNetwork to handle:
      - Larger input (20 sensors vs 5)
      - Continuous action output (Gaussian vs Categorical)
      - Actor-Critic architecture (required for PPO)
      - Deeper sparse network

  ARCHITECTURE:
    Input (20)
       ↓
    MaskedLinear(20 → 64, sparsity=0.8)
       ↓ ReLU
    MaskedLinear(64 → 64, sparsity=0.8)
       ↓ ReLU
       ├── Actor head: MaskedLinear(64 → 4) → Tanh → action mean
       │              + log_std parameter → Gaussian distribution
       └── Critic head: MaskedLinear(64 → 1) → state value V(s)

  ACTIVE SYNAPSE COUNT:
    Layer 1: 20 × 64 × 0.2  = ~256 active
    Layer 2: 64 × 64 × 0.2  = ~819 active
    Actor:   64 × 4          = 256  active
    Critic:  64 × 1          = 64   active
    Total:   ~1395 active synapses out of ~6976 possible
    Still only 20% connected — biologically constrained

  SUCCESS CRITERIA:
    - Forward pass produces valid Gaussian distribution
    - Critic outputs scalar value estimate
    - Gradient check: severed synapses still have zero gradient

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  PHASE 8 — Domain Randomization
  File: domain_rand.py
  Hardware: M1 MacBook Air
  Estimated Time: 1 session
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  GOAL:
    Make the trained policy robust to sim-to-real gap by randomizing
    every aspect of the simulation that might differ from reality.

  THIS IS THE SIM-TO-REAL BRIDGE:
    The idea: if the policy works across a HUGE range of simulated
    conditions, it will probably work in at least one real condition.

  WHAT WE RANDOMIZE:
    Environment:
      - Tunnel radius          : uniform(25, 100)
      - Curve intensity        : uniform(0.05, 1.0)
      - Curve smoothness       : uniform(0.02, 0.15)
      - Tunnel length          : uniform(500, 2000) steps
      - Obstacle density       : uniform(0, 0.3)

    Sensors:
      - Gaussian noise sigma   : uniform(0, 0.08)
      - Sensor dropout prob    : uniform(0, 0.1)
      - Sensor delay (steps)   : randint(0, 3)
      - Max range variation    : uniform(0.8, 1.2) × nominal

    Physics:
      - Gravity scale          : uniform(0.8, 1.2)
      - Drag coefficient       : uniform(0.7, 1.3)
      - Motor response time    : uniform(0.8, 1.2)
      - Mass                   : uniform(0.8, 1.2) × nominal

    Initial conditions:
      - Start position jitter  : uniform(-10, 10) all axes
      - Start velocity         : uniform(-2, 2) all axes
      - Start orientation      : uniform(-15°, 15°) all axes

  SUCCESS CRITERIA:
    - Policy trained WITH domain rand generalizes to unseen tunnel configs
    - Performance gap < 20% between easiest and hardest configs

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  PHASE 9 — Real FlyWire Connectome Integration
  File: connectome.py
  Hardware: M1 MacBook Air
  Estimated Time: 2-3 sessions
  Dependencies: cloudvolume, navis, fafbseg
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  THIS IS THE CORE SCIENTIFIC CONTRIBUTION

  DATA SOURCE:
    FlyWire Consortium, Princeton University
    Paper: "Neuronal wiring diagram of an adult brain" (Nature, 2023)
    URL  : fly.flywire.ai
    Data : ~130,000 neurons, ~50,000,000 synapses, fully mapped

  WHICH NEURONS WE EXTRACT:
    We only care about the visual navigation subsystem:

    Lamina (L1, L2, L3):
      - First stage visual processing
      - Detect edges, contrast, motion onset
      - ~400 neurons

    Medulla (T4, T5):
      - Motion direction selective neurons
      - T4: ON motion (light → dark)
      - T5: OFF motion (dark → light)
      - ~1200 neurons total
      - Critical for optic flow sensing

    Lobula Plate (LPLC2):
      - Looming object detector
      - Fires when something approaches on collision course
      - ~20 neurons
      - Directly relevant to wall avoidance

    Descending Neurons (DNp09, DNa02, others):
      - Connect brain to motor system
      - ~50 neurons
      - These ARE the action outputs

  PIPELINE:
    Step 1: Download neuron IDs for above cell types from FlyWire
    Step 2: Query synapse table for connections BETWEEN these neurons
    Step 3: Build adjacency matrix A where A[i,j] = synapse count
    Step 4: Threshold: keep connections with > N synapses
    Step 5: Scale adjacency matrix to match our network dimensions
            using hierarchical clustering / dimensionality reduction
    Step 6: Use scaled matrix as the MaskedLinear mask
    Step 7: Train with PPO, weights learned, topology fixed

  THE KEY EXPERIMENT:
    Train 3 identical models, same everything EXCEPT the mask:
      Model A: Dense (no mask)             — baseline
      Model B: Random sparse (80%)         — current approach
      Model C: FlyWire connectome topology — our hypothesis

    Compare:
      - Sample efficiency (episodes to reach reward threshold)
      - Generalization (performance on unseen tunnel configs)
      - Parameter efficiency (reward per active synapse)
      - Robustness to sensor noise

  SUCCESS CRITERIA:
    - Connectome mask loads without errors
    - Model C trains successfully with biological topology
    - At least one metric where C > B > A (or interesting failure!)

════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  SECTION 5: RESEARCH QUESTIONS & HYPOTHESES
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  RQ1: Can a biologically-constrained sparse network learn drone navigation?
       H1: Yes, sparse connectome networks (≥80% severed) can achieve
           comparable performance to dense networks in simulated navigation.

  RQ2: Does biological topology outperform random sparsity?
       H2: FlyWire connectome topology will show better sample efficiency
           than random sparse masks of equivalent density, because biological
           wiring encodes useful computational motifs (edge detection,
           motion detection, looming response).

  RQ3: What is the minimum viable connectome for navigation?
       H3: A network with ≥95% sparsity (only 5% connections active) can
           still learn basic obstacle avoidance, but generalization requires
           at least 80% sparsity or better.

  RQ4: Does sparse topology improve sim-to-real transfer?
       H4: Models trained with domain randomization + sparse connectome
           will show smaller performance degradation when tested on
           out-of-distribution environments than dense baselines.

  RQ5: What biological circuit motifs are most important for navigation?
       H5: The looming detection circuit (LPLC2) will be most critical —
           ablating it from the connectome mask will cause disproportionate
           performance loss in collision avoidance.

  SECTION 7: TECHNICAL ARCHITECTURE DECISIONS
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  DECISION 1: Why not use AirSim/Isaac Gym?
    - Both require 6GB+ RAM (M1 Air has 8GB total, OS uses 2-3GB)
    - AirSim is Windows/Linux primary, Mac support is unstable
    - Isaac Gym requires NVIDIA GPU
    - Custom env gives full control over domain randomization
    - Can wrap AirSim LATER once algorithm is proven

  DECISION 2: Why PyTorch over JAX?
    - MPS (Apple Metal) backend for M1 GPU acceleration
    - Better ecosystem for RL (stable-baselines3 compatible)
    - Easier debugging with eager execution
    - JAX would be faster on TPU but we don't have TPU access

  DECISION 3: Why PPO over SAC/TD3?
    - PPO works well with sparse rewards (common in navigation)
    - SAC requires dense rewards or reward shaping
    - PPO is on-policy: simpler to implement from scratch
    - TD3 is off-policy: needs large replay buffer (memory pressure on M1)

  DECISION 4: Why discrete → continuous action upgrade?
    - Phase 1-4: Discrete(3) for simplicity and fast iteration
    - Phase 5+: Box(4) because real drones have continuous controls
    - The switch happens WITH the 3D env upgrade (Phase 5-7)
    - Can always discretize continuous actions for hardware safety

  DECISION 5: Why fixed mask vs learned sparsity?
    - Fixed mask = biologically realistic (fly brain is fixed at birth)
    - Learned sparsity (lottery ticket, L1 pruning) is different question
    - Our hypothesis is specifically about TOPOLOGY as inductive bias
    - Learned sparsity would conflate two separate variables
    - We DO want a separate experiment: does the mask need to be fixed?
